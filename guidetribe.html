GUIDETRIBE

A trip planning application for ios

  • Problem

    There is a lot to look forward to when planning a trip, but planning on your own can be time-consuming and overwhelming. In addition to having to sift through the seemingly endless amount of sights to explore and prioritizing, there is the added task of pooling it all into a harmonious itinerary.

  • Solution

    An application that makes exploring new cities easier by crowdsourcing reliable walking routes and itineraries from those who know these cities the best - locals and past visitors.

PHASE 1 : DISCOVERY AND DEVELOPMENT

Survey/Interviews

Before delving into the design of GuideTribe, I needed to better understand:

  1. whether there was a need for a tool like this,
  2. who would use this tool, and
  3. what types of features the tool should include

I conducted two surveys and one round of interviews.

SURVEY HIGHLIGHTS

  • 100% of respondents use a mobile device to learn more about sights they are seeing, and to navigate the city they are visiting.
  • 72% of respondents reported that they asked locals for their first hand recommendations.
  • 72% of respondents create pre-planned daily itineraries of sights and activities.
  • The majority of respondents used Tripadvisor and Google Maps to plan their trips.
  • The top 5 sightseeing categories sought after were Historic/Cultural Monuments, Restaurants/Food, Neighborhoods, Museums and “off the beaten path.

INTERVIEW FINDINGS

During my user interviews, I asked questions that probed into past planning experiences and behaviors. These interviews revealed some major pain points while planning:

  1. Planning takes a significant effort since it takes time to research sightseeing destinations and to consolidate it all into a well thought out itinerary.
  2. There isn’t a centralized tool that allows one to plot destinations on a map, determine route length and timing, and to discover more information about these destinations.
  3. General research typically only produces “touristy” results but is void of “local” recommendations.
  4. It is difficult to use existing tools like Tripadvisor and Google Maps when there is no access to WIFI or cellular service.
  5. Current methods of route navigation are limited to plotting locations in Google Maps point by point.

GOALS IDENTIFIED BY PERSONAS

  1. Reduce trip planning time
  2. A way to build personalized itinerary of sights
  3. A way to consolidate trip information into one application

Based on these findings, it was clear that a need for a mobile based tool that facilitated sightseeing trip planning existed. It was also clear that sightseers could benefit from an application that sourced route and location recommendations from past visitors and locals.

Competitive Analysis

I created a SWOT matrix combining data from four competitor products: Google Trips, Detour, LiveTrekker and SideKix.

Google Trips

Offers the ability to build custom maps and store trip activity details in one place but is clunky and inflexible. The location database is limited and has no ability to add custom locations.

Detour

Offers pre-made walking maps that are synced with audio, but maps are not customizeable and limited in international cities.

LiveTrekker

Offers the ability to discover trip itineraries submitted by the community and curated by the LiveTrekker team, but the map function is unreliable.

SideKix

Offers the ability to create walking routes based on your interests but many icon functions are not immediately cleas which can compromise user experience and retention.

PHASE 2 : WIREFRAMING AND TESTING

Wireframes

I translated my design notes and site maps into sketches to define the placement of elements on each page.

Next, I developed low-fidelity wireframes in Balsamiq that I used to build a clickable prototype in Invision to conduct preliminary testing with potential users.

I then created high fidelity wireframes and a prototype using Figma that included written content for each screen.

Usability Testing

I conducted three rounds of user testing on my high fidelity wireframes.

USABILITY TEST #1 | Using Maze

I used Maze to conduct my first round of testing. During the test, participants were asked to:

  1. Sign-up for an account
  2. Set-up an account
  3. Conduct a search for routes in New York City
  4. Create a custom route
  5. Draw a custom map

Of the 3 total participants, 2 had failed to complete the task that asked them to create a custom route. Specifically, participants had the most difficulty with figuring out how to draw their custom map once destinations were added to it.

The image below displays the activity of a participant that struggled with drawing their custom route. Ultimately, this user gave up.

In light of this data, it was clear that the creation of a custom route needed resolving, especially since it is a core function of the application.

USABILITY TEST #2 | In-person Testing

In an effort to solve this problem, I conducted 2 in-person usability tests to delve deeper into the issues that stood in the way of success. Several insights came out of these in-person tests:

  1. The process for adding location pins via the search function was not obvious.
  2. The “draw” icon did not match mental models.
  3. Even when clicking on the “draw” icon to activate the function, linking locations required numerous clicks and did not provide options that allowed:
    • The selection of start/end locations
    • Modification of location order
    • Deletion of a location

This feedback proved that creating a custom map needed to be simpler with less steps, and provide prompts that allowed for the more intuitive functions shared during the in-person usability tests. In response to this feedback, I re-imagined this area. Since incorporating these features into the “map build” made for a more complex experience, I replaced the “map build” with a “list build.” Users would generate a list of locations that can be re-ordered by dragging, and edited within the list view. This list can then be viewed on a map in its listed order.

USABILITY TEST #3 | In-person Testing

I conducted a third round of in-person testing with 3 users to evaluate the usability of the updated prototype. I utilized the “record” feature on my iphone to document these usability tests. Each test took approximately 10 minutes.

Users were asked to complete the following tasks:

  1. Sign-up for an account
  2. Search for and select a route in New York City
  3. Create a custom route

The updates that were tested during the third usability test revealed improvement to users ability to create a custom route. This was evident by the unanimous rating of 4 out of 5 for ease of use. Specifically, users were pleased with the ease when searching for and modifying their custom route locations.

PHASE 3 : VISUAL DESIGN

Branding

Having completed user testing, I developed a brand identity to incorporate into my final design mockups.

The brand name was developed through mindmapping. The main concept for the application is to crowdsource sightseeing routes from those that know cities the best - locals and past visitors. These groups make up a community resource - your “tribe” of “guides.”

GuideTribe's logo represents the overall brand and its values. The final design was influenced by the World Triad as it is a universal representation of inter-dependance, inter-connectedness and community. The individual shapes mirror location markers. The formation mimics the shape repetition we typically see in tribal patterns across the world.

In considering the color scheme for GuideTribe, I turned to my own love of vintage maps. I scoured the internet for examples that reflected the vivid and contrasting tones inherent in these old maps. I selected the colors that were consistent among these maps - shades of teal, pink, yellow and orange.
